# Blank Extraction Guidelines

## Goal
Take a fact and convert it into a fill-in-the-blank format by identifying and removing the most surprising or unexpected detail.

## The Blank

The blank represents the **most unexpected/bizarre detail** in the fact — the part the reader would never predict, the thing that makes them think "wait, really?"

### Format
Use `[blank]` as the placeholder.

### Length
- **Always aim for 1–3 words** — this is a hard preference, not a soft guideline
- If the surprising detail is a longer clause, restructure the fact to push surrounding words in, leaving only the core 1–3 word nugget as the blank
- A longer phrase is acceptable only when the surprise genuinely cannot be condensed without losing its meaning
- A full sentence is never acceptable

### Tone
- **Lean informal** — casual, conversational, even slangy phrasing makes the blank punchier and harder to predict from a stiff paraphrase
- Prefer "totally lost it" over "became extremely agitated"; "wrote an actual paper about it" over "published a scientific paper"; "bailed" over "abandoned the premises"
- Match the energy of the fact: absurd stories deserve loose, punchy answers

### Position
The blank can appear **anywhere** in the sentence — beginning, middle, or end — wherever the surprising detail naturally falls.

## How to Identify the Best Blank

Ask yourself: **which detail would make someone stop mid-sentence and say "wait, really?"**

- It's usually the core absurd/shocking act — not its consequence, explanation, or aftermath
- It's the thing that feels out of place or impossible in context
- If you removed it, the fact would lose its "wow" entirely
- When multiple details surprise, pick the one that's hardest to predict from the rest of the sentence
- **Never skip because a post has too many options** — multiple surprising details is a reason to pick the best one, not to skip

## Rules

1. **The core absurd act, not its consequence** — prefer the shocking act itself over its explanation or aftermath. Exception: if the motivation or reason is clearly more bizarre than the act (e.g. "blaming voices in his head"), blank the motivation instead and leave the act visible
2. **Blank the specific detail, not the action** — when the surprise is about *what* something was done to/with/into, keep the action in the fact and blank only the specific unexpected object, destination, or target. E.g. "drove it into [blank]" not "drove into [blank]" or "[blank]" alone. The blank should answer "what?" not "what did he do?"
3. **Rewrite to protect the surprise** — if the surrounding text makes the answer too obvious, rephrase or omit that part. The goal is that the reader cannot guess the blank from context alone
4. **Push framing words into the fact, not the blank** — when a blank starts with a connective like "that", "because", "who", or includes a subject, restructure the fact to absorb those words and keep only the core 1–3 word predicate in the blank. E.g. fact: "...because the leader feared they would [blank]", blank: "overthrow the regime" — not blank: "the leader feared they would overthrow the regime". Apply the same when the blank contains a subject: fact: "...כי המנהיג חושש ששלישיות [blank]", blank: "יפילו את המשטר" — not blank: "שלישיות יפילו את המשטר". This also applies when the blank contains a less-surprising part alongside the real punchline — push the less-surprising part into the fact:
   - fact: "בשנת 1969 נשים הפגינו בעד זכות [blank]", blank: "השתנה ברחוב" — not blank: "בעד זכות השתנה ברחוב" ("for the right to" is framing, not the surprise)
   - fact: "בסיס מודיעין נטוש בברלין הפך לגלריית אמנות ול[blank]", blank: "חלל מסיבות" — not blank: "הפך לגלריית אמנות וחלל מסיבות" ("became an art gallery" is context; "party venue" is the punchline)
5. **Avoid bare names or numbers as the blank** — a standalone number (e.g. "28") or proper name (e.g. "Cher") makes a poor blank. The answer should carry enough context to feel satisfying — "three cats" or "published a scientific paper about it" works; "28" or "Cher" alone does not
6. **Illegal or sexual details are almost always the blank** — if the post contains a specific illegal item, drug, weapon, or sexual act, that is usually the blank. The action stays in the fact; the specific surprising thing is what gets hidden. E.g. "Florida Man hides 15 sticks of stolen deodorant in his pants" → fact: "Florida Man hid 15 [blank] in his pants", blank: "sticks of deodorant" (the number stays in the fact to add scale; the absurd object is the blank). "Florida Man Dealing Drugs While Wearing 'I Have Drugs' T-Shirt" → blank: "'I Have Drugs'" (the specific incriminating detail). This applies even when the illegality is obvious from context — blank the specific thing, not the act.
7. **Headline-only posts are valid — almost never skip them** — if the post is just a title or headline with no body, do NOT skip it. Extract the blank from the headline itself. The surprising object, weapon, disguise, method, or twist is the blank; the action stays in the fact. Examples:
   - "Florida Man Attacks Coworker with Machete" → fact: "Florida Man attacks coworker with [blank]", blank: "machete"
   - "Florida Man Robs Store Dressed as Spiderman" → fact: "Florida Man robs store dressed as [blank]", blank: "Spiderman"
   - "Florida Man Eats Crack Cocaine; Tells Police It's Jolly Rancher Candy" → fact: "Florida Man eats crack cocaine, tells police it's [blank]", blank: "Jolly Rancher Candy"
   - "Florida Man And Girlfriend Trapped in Unlocked Closet for 2 Days" → fact: "Florida Man and girlfriend were trapped for 2 days in a closet that was [blank]", blank: "unlocked"
   - "Florida Man sues for right to marry his laptop" → fact: "Florida Man sues for the right to marry [blank]", blank: "his laptop"
   - "Florida Man Dealing Drugs While Wearing 'I Have Drugs' T-Shirt" → fact: "Florida Man was caught dealing drugs while wearing a shirt that said [blank]", blank: "'I Have Drugs'"
   - "Florida man eats meth to avoid arrest, doesn't work" → fact: "Florida Man ate [blank] to avoid arrest — and it didn't work", blank: "meth" (the surprising object is what he ate, not the consequence; keep the twist in the fact)
   - Only skip if there is genuinely no specific surprising detail: pure site boilerplate ("Privacy Policy", "Discussion Guidelines"), vague listicle titles ("Top 10", "7 Times Florida Man..."), or meta pages ("Who is Florida Man?", "floridaman.com")

## Examples

### Example 1 — Cher/Thatcher hashtag
**Original:** When Margaret Thatcher died, the hashtag #nowthatchersdead spread online. Because of how it was read, websites began publishing obituaries for Cher.

**With blank:** When Margaret Thatcher died, the hashtag #nowthatchersdead spread online — and because of how it was read, websites began [blank].

**Answer:** publishing obituaries for Cher

---

### Example 2 — Roberto Carlos
**Original:** Roberto Carlos's 1997 free kick was so impressive that two physicists published a scientific paper about it in 2010.

**With blank:** Roberto Carlos's 1997 free kick was so impressive that two physicists [blank].

**Answer:** published a scientific paper about it

---

### Example 3 — Dali Atomicus
**Original:** "Dali Atomicus" — one of the most iconic photos in history — was captured using buckets of water, three cats, and 28 attempts over 6 hours.

**With blank:** "Dali Atomicus" was captured with no digital technology — just buckets of water, [blank], and 28 attempts over 6 hours.

**Answer:** three cats

---

### Example 4 — Friends theme song
**Original:** The Friends theme song started as just a 45-second verse. Fans started requesting it on the radio, so a DJ looped it, and the Rembrandts eventually recorded a full song.

**With blank:** The Friends theme song started as just a 45-second verse. Fans started [blank], so a DJ looped it into a full song.

**Answer:** requesting it on the radio

---

### Example 5 — Dogs don't sweat
**Original:** Dogs cool themselves through panting, not sweating. Their sweat glands are located only in their paw pads.

**With blank:** Dogs cool themselves through panting — their sweat glands are only in their [blank].

**Answer:** paw pads

---

### Example 6 — Football murder 1975
**Original:** During a 1975 football match, a fan entered the pitch and stabbed and killed a player mid-game. The offending club was permanently disbanded as punishment.

**With blank:** During a 1975 football match, a fan entered the pitch and [blank]. The offending club was permanently disbanded as punishment.

**Answer:** stabbed and killed a player

---

### Example 7 — Texas elections 1952
**Original:** In the 1952 Texas elections, a candidate ran against himself representing both the Democratic and Republican parties — and won with 73%.

**With blank:** In the 1952 Texas elections, a candidate [blank] — and won with 73%.

**Answer:** ran against himself representing both parties

---

### Example 8 — Skydiving camera rule
**Original:** The US Parachute Association requires skydivers to complete 200 jumps before being allowed to attach a camera to their helmet — because beginners get distracted and forget to check their altitude.

**With blank:** The US Parachute Association requires skydivers to complete 200 jumps before being allowed to [blank] — because beginners get distracted and forget to check their altitude.

**Answer:** attach a camera to their helmet

---

### Example 9 — Florida man, voices
**Original:** A Florida man robbed a convenience store, blaming voices in his head.

**With blank:** A Florida man robbed a convenience store, blaming [blank].

**Answer:** voices in his head

*Note: the act (robbing a store) is less surprising than the reason — so the reason is blanked, not the act.*

---

### Example 10 — Stolen ambulance
**Original:** A Florida man stole an ambulance from a hospital and drove it into mud.

**With blank:** A Florida man stole an ambulance from a hospital and drove it into [blank].

**Answer:** mud

*Note: the surprising detail is the destination, not the act of driving. Keep the action in the fact and blank only the specific unexpected object/destination.*

---

### Example 11 — North Korea triplets

**Original:** בצפון קוריאה, כאשר נולדת שלישייה, הממשלה מעבירה אותה למוסד. המנהיג העליון משוכנע ששלישייה עלולה להפיל את משטרו, כך שהמשטר מחליט לפקח עליהם ולגדל אותם על אהבת המנהיג.

**With blank:** בצפון קוריאה, כאשר נולדת שלישייה, הממשלה מעבירה אותה למוסד — כי המנהיג חושש שהם יכולים [blank].

**Answer:** יפילו את המשטר

*Note: the surprising detail is the core fear (overthrowing the regime), not the whole motivation clause. Push all framing ("כי המנהיג חושש שהם יכולים") into the fact; the blank is only the core 3-word predicate. Wrong: blank = "המנהיג חושש שהם יפילו את משטרו" — that includes the subject and framing, which belong in the fact.*

---

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] Is the blank the most unexpected detail in the fact?
- [ ] Would a reader fail to predict the answer from context alone?
- [ ] Is the blank a word or short phrase (not a full sentence unless necessary)?
- [ ] Does the surrounding text stay mostly intact?
- [ ] Is the blank formatted as `[blank]`?
