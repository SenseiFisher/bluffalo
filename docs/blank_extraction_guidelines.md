# Blank Extraction Guidelines

## Goal
Take a fact and convert it into a fill-in-the-blank format by identifying and removing the most surprising or unexpected detail.

## The Blank

The blank represents the **most unexpected/bizarre detail** in the fact — the part the reader would never predict, the thing that makes them think "wait, really?"

### Format
Use `[blank]` as the placeholder.

### Length
- Prefer a **word or short phrase**
- A full sentence is acceptable only if the surprise cannot be captured in less

### Position
The blank can appear **anywhere** in the sentence — beginning, middle, or end — wherever the surprising detail naturally falls.

## How to Identify the Best Blank

Ask yourself: **which detail would make someone stop mid-sentence and say "wait, really?"**

- It's usually the core absurd/shocking act — not its consequence, explanation, or aftermath
- It's the thing that feels out of place or impossible in context
- If you removed it, the fact would lose its "wow" entirely
- When multiple details surprise, pick the one that's hardest to predict from the rest of the sentence

## Rules

1. **The core absurd act, not its consequence** — prefer the shocking act itself over its explanation or aftermath
2. **Rewrite to protect the surprise** — if the surrounding text makes the answer too obvious, rephrase or omit that part. The goal is that the reader cannot guess the blank from context alone
3. **Avoid bare names or numbers as the blank** — a standalone number (e.g. "28") or proper name (e.g. "Cher") makes a poor blank. The answer should carry enough context to feel satisfying — "three cats" or "published a scientific paper about it" works; "28" or "Cher" alone does not

## Examples

### Example 1 — Cher/Thatcher hashtag
**Original:** When Margaret Thatcher died, the hashtag #nowthatchersdead spread online. Because of how it was read, websites began publishing obituaries for Cher.

**With blank:** When Margaret Thatcher died, the hashtag #nowthatchersdead spread online — and because of how it was read, websites began [blank].

**Answer:** publishing obituaries for Cher

---

### Example 2 — Roberto Carlos
**Original:** Roberto Carlos's 1997 free kick was so impressive that two physicists published a scientific paper about it in 2010.

**With blank:** Roberto Carlos's 1997 free kick was so impressive that two physicists [blank].

**Answer:** published a scientific paper about it

---

### Example 3 — Dali Atomicus
**Original:** "Dali Atomicus" — one of the most iconic photos in history — was captured using buckets of water, three cats, and 28 attempts over 6 hours.

**With blank:** "Dali Atomicus" was captured with no digital technology — just buckets of water, [blank], and 28 attempts over 6 hours.

**Answer:** three cats

---

### Example 4 — Friends theme song
**Original:** The Friends theme song started as just a 45-second verse. Fans started requesting it on the radio, so a DJ looped it, and the Rembrandts eventually recorded a full song.

**With blank:** The Friends theme song started as just a 45-second verse. Fans started [blank], so a DJ looped it into a full song.

**Answer:** requesting it on the radio

---

### Example 5 — Dogs don't sweat
**Original:** Dogs cool themselves through panting, not sweating. Their sweat glands are located only in their paw pads.

**With blank:** Dogs cool themselves through panting — their sweat glands are only in their [blank].

**Answer:** paw pads

---

### Example 6 — Football murder 1975
**Original:** During a 1975 football match, a fan entered the pitch and stabbed and killed a player mid-game. The offending club was permanently disbanded as punishment.

**With blank:** During a 1975 football match, a fan entered the pitch and [blank]. The offending club was permanently disbanded as punishment.

**Answer:** stabbed and killed a player

---

### Example 7 — Texas elections 1952
**Original:** In the 1952 Texas elections, a candidate ran against himself representing both the Democratic and Republican parties — and won with 73%.

**With blank:** In the 1952 Texas elections, a candidate [blank] — and won with 73%.

**Answer:** ran against himself representing both parties

---

### Example 8 — Skydiving camera rule
**Original:** The US Parachute Association requires skydivers to complete 200 jumps before being allowed to attach a camera to their helmet — because beginners get distracted and forget to check their altitude.

**With blank:** The US Parachute Association requires skydivers to complete 200 jumps before being allowed to [blank] — because beginners get distracted and forget to check their altitude.

**Answer:** attach a camera to their helmet

---

## Quick Checklist

- [ ] Is the blank the most unexpected detail in the fact?
- [ ] Would a reader fail to predict the answer from context alone?
- [ ] Is the blank a word or short phrase (not a full sentence unless necessary)?
- [ ] Does the surrounding text stay mostly intact?
- [ ] Is the blank formatted as `[blank]`?
